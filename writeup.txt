How Good is Instant Runoff Voting? — Design Write-Up

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DESIGN RATIONALE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The central research question is whether Instant Runoff Voting (IRV)
consistently elects broadly preferred candidates. The Condorcet criterion
offers a precise test: a "Condorcet winner" is a candidate who would beat
every other candidate in a head-to-head matchup. IRV can, in principle,
eliminate that candidate before the final round. The visualization
classifies every real-world IRV election in the dataset into one of four
outcomes: (1) IRV and plurality agree on the Condorcet winner (No Change,
green); (2) IRV elects the Condorcet winner but plurality would not
(Improvement, blue); (3) IRV eliminates the Condorcet winner (Failure,
yellow); (4) no Condorcet winner exists due to cyclic preferences (Cycle,
purple).

Visual encoding. The four categories map to four semantically charged
colors. Green and blue signal that IRV is working as intended; yellow
(warning) and purple (anomaly) flag failures. I deliberately avoided a
gradient: these categories are theoretically distinct, not points on a
spectrum of "goodness." Each jurisdiction appears as a dot on an Albers
USA projection — a familiar, area-preserving map for US audiences. Dot
color is determined by the worst outcome across all elections in that
jurisdiction, so a city with even one Condorcet failure is marked yellow.
Dot opacity drops to near-zero for categories the user has filtered out,
letting the remaining data stay in focus without a full redraw.

Interaction hierarchy. The design moves from overview to detail. The stats
strip at the top establishes scale (606 elections, 95% green) before the
user explores. Clicking a dot zooms the map to that city and opens a side
panel listing elections as cards. Clicking a card opens a fullscreen Sankey
diagram showing vote flows through each IRV elimination round. This
three-level hierarchy — map → panel → Sankey — lets casual users get the
headline story and expert users drill into ballot-level mechanics.

Sankey design choices. The Sankey diagram was the central alternative
worth considering. Bar charts would only show final-round totals; a table
of round-by-round tallies would require active reading. The Sankey's link
widths make vote transfers immediately visible — you can see a small
candidate's supporters redistributing to a major candidate in one glance.
I used close-side entry (links enter and exit nodes near their vertical
source) to reduce visual crossing. Write-in candidates and zero-vote
candidates are filtered out to keep the diagram legible. For yellow and
purple elections a second panel appears alongside: a hub-and-spoke layout
for Condorcet failure cases, and a three-node cycle diagram for preference
cycles, both showing raw head-to-head vote counts.

Clustering. Several jurisdictions (Bay Area cities, Twin Cities suburbs,
Utah Valley towns) are geographically close enough to overlap at national
zoom. Clicking a merged cluster opens a city-picker before showing
elections, preventing ambiguity about which city's data is displayed.

Alternatives rejected. I considered a choropleth of states colored by
failure rate, but IRV is adopted city-by-city, not state-by-state, so a
dot map preserves the correct unit of analysis. I also considered a
force-directed network of pairwise preferences, but it would be
unreadable for elections with many candidates and would obscure the
temporal (round-by-round) structure of IRV.


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DEVELOPMENT PROCESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Total development time was approximately 12 hours across research,
data processing, visualization coding, and design iteration.

The project began with a literature search to find a suitable dataset.
Claude (Anthropic) assisted in identifying relevant academic sources and
IRV datasets; every reference was manually verified against the actual
publication. The primary data source is:

  Otis, Deb. 2022. "Single Winner Ranked Choice Voting CVRs."
  Harvard Dataverse. https://doi.org/10.7910/DVN/AMK8PJ

This dataset contains cast vote record (CVR) CSV files for 607 real
ranked-choice elections across 31 US jurisdictions.

I set up the project structure, HTML template, and overall visual
design myself. Claude and GitHub Copilot wrote the bulk of the
data-processing pipeline (build_real_data.py) — including the IRV
simulator, pairwise Condorcet computation, and Sankey node/link
builder — as well as much of the D3 plumbing (zoom behavior, dot
rendering, clustering logic, tooltip positioning). I directed this
work through iterative prompting, reviewed every output, and made
all design and storytelling decisions: which encodings to use, what
story to tell, how panels should be structured, and what level of
detail to expose at each step.

Working this way felt comfortable. It is most similar to pair
programming with a fast, knowledgeable partner who does not have
opinions about aesthetics or narrative framing. The most time-
consuming parts were: (1) iterating on the Sankey layout to handle
link crossing and close-side entry correctly; (2) designing the
pairwise winner and cycle panels so that raw vote counts were
legible without overwhelming the diagram; and (3) the label
collision-avoidance algorithm for city name placement on the map.
These required multiple rounds of feedback and adjustment that a
non-interactive code generator could not short-circuit.

Libraries used: D3.js v7.8.5, d3-sankey v0.12.3, TopoJSON v3.0.2,
us-atlas v3 (Albers USA state boundaries).
